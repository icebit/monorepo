# Eating Philosophy

## Core Principle

Eat close to the earth. Prioritize whole, minimally processed foods rooted in
Mediterranean and Indian culinary traditions. The system should reduce friction
to the point where eating well is the path of least resistance — not willpower.

## Health Constraint: Familial Hypercholesterolemia

This is non-negotiable and shapes every food decision:

- **Minimize saturated fat.** Red meat, full-fat dairy, cheese, and butter are
  not staples — they are rare exceptions at most.
- **Favor unsaturated fats.** Olive oil, nuts, seeds, avocado, fatty fish.
- **Emphasize soluble fiber.** Lentils, oats, beans, barley — these actively
  help pull cholesterol down.
- **Lean proteins.** Chicken, fish, legumes, tofu. Lentils do double duty as
  protein and fiber.

Even with medication, diet is a lever worth pulling hard on.

## Food Identity

What "eating well" actually looks and tastes like:

- **Indian food is the backbone.** Dal (red lentil, chana, moong), rogan josh
  made lean (lamb in moderation or substituted with chicken), saag, chana
  masala, rajma. These are hearty, flavorful, and naturally align with the
  health constraints. Most Indian staples are legume-heavy, veggie-forward, and
  use spices rather than dairy for richness.
- **Mediterranean staples fill the gaps.** Olive oil as the default fat.
  Hummus, tabbouleh, roasted vegetables, grain bowls. Fresh and simple.
- **Smoothies as a daily vehicle.** Spinach/kale, frozen fruit, flax/chia,
  protein powder, oat milk. A reliable way to front-load nutrients with near
  zero friction.
- **Whole grains over refined.** Brown rice, quinoa, whole wheat roti, barley.
- **Vegetables are not a side dish.** They are the main event or at least equal
  partners. Roasted, raw, in curries, in salads — volume matters.

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

Things that pull the system off track:

- **Defaulting to dad's cooking when it's heavy.** Steak nights, cheesy
  dishes, and microwaved Costco sides don't align. Need a way to gracefully
  opt out or supplement on those nights.
- **Cava as the only lunch option.** It's fine as a fallback, but the goal is
  to bring lunch most days. The system has to make grab-and-go realistic.
- **Grocery atrophy.** Not shopping means not cooking means not eating well.
  The shopping habit is the root of the whole chain.
- **Dirty kitchen friction.** A messy shared kitchen kills motivation to cook.
  The system can't fix the kitchen, but it can favor meals that minimize
  dishes and cleanup.

## Design Principles for the System

1. **Reduce decisions, not options.** The system should suggest what to eat and
   what to buy. Override anytime, but the default path should be clear.
2. **Prep cadence is adaptive.** No rigid "Sunday meal prep" mandate. Track
   what actually gets done and adjust. Maybe batch cooking works, maybe daily
   15-minute cooking works, maybe it alternates. Let the data decide.
3. **Smoothies are the anchor.** One meal per day that requires almost no
   thought and is always healthy. Build the habit here first.
4. **Lunch is portable by design.** Meals that work at room temp or reheated
   in a microwave. Grain bowls, dal with rice, wraps. Things that go into a
   container the night before.
5. **Minimize dishes.** One-pot meals, sheet pan meals, meals eaten from the
   container they were stored in. Respect the shared kitchen constraint.
6. **Stock a pantry that doesn't expire.** Dried lentils, canned beans, rice,
   frozen vegetables, spices, oats, frozen fruit, olive oil. If the pantry is
   stocked, a decent meal is always 20 minutes away even without a grocery
   trip that week.
7. **Don't moralize food.** The system isn't about guilt. It's about making
   the healthy path easier than the unhealthy one. Cava on a busy day is
   fine. Eating dad's dinner sometimes is fine. The goal is shifting the
   overall ratio, not perfection.
